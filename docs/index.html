Civil War and Household Structure: Evidence from Burundi

Technical Supplement and Replication Materials

Abstract

This paper examines how civil war affects household structure by analyzing individual and household migration decisions in Burundi between 1998 and 2007. Using a unique longitudinal household panel dataset covering 872 rural households and 3,480 household members, we exploit within-individual and within-household variation in conflict exposure across time and geography. We distinguish between non-marital (economic) migration and marital migration as distinct responses to conflict. We find that village-level conflict exposure increases non-marital migration by 3.1 percentage points, driven primarily by asset and crop losses inflicted on households. In contrast, marital migration is not affected by village-level violence but is sensitive to household victimization through asset losses. Conflict also substantially reduces the probability of return migration, implying durable changes to household composition. These results highlight that civil war reshapes family structure through multiple channels, with households deploying migration as a risk-coping strategy in response to direct victimization.

Empirical Strategy

The core identification strategy exploits within-individual (or within-household) variation in village-level conflict exposure over time:

\[\text{Leave}_{iht} = \alpha_i + \delta_t + \gamma_{p(h)} \cdot t + \beta \cdot \text{Conflict}_{v(h)t} + \varepsilon_{iht}\]

where:

  • \(\alpha_i\) = individual fixed effect (absorbs all time-invariant heterogeneity)
  • \(\delta_t\) = year fixed effect (absorbs aggregate national trends)
  • \(\gamma_{p(h)} \cdot t\) = province-specific time trend (controls for differential regional trajectories)
  • \(\text{Conflict}_{v(h)t}\) = village-level conflict exposure (binary or continuous intensity)
  • Standard errors clustered at the village (sous-colline) level

The individual fixed effect eliminates confounding from time-invariant characteristics such as ethnicity, pre-war socioeconomic status, and location. Identification relies on whether the same individual migrates more in years when their village experiences conflict than in years without conflict.

Related Literature

This paper contributes to three main literatures:

Economics of Conflict: Blattman & Miguel (2010) provide a comprehensive review of the economic consequences of civil war. Vesco et al. (2025) review the impacts of armed conflict on human development. Collier (1999) and Deininger (2003) examine micro-level economic consequences of civil strife.

Household Risk and Migration: Morduch (1995) and Rosenzweig & Stark (1989) develop the theoretical framework for migration as a household risk-coping strategy. Lauby & Stark (1988) apply this to women’s migration in the Philippines.

Burundi-Specific Evidence: Mercier et al. (2020) study the effect of violence exposure on household poverty using the same dataset. Verwimp & Bundervoet (2009) examine household welfare in extended households. Bozzoli et al. (2013) study self-employment and conflict in Colombia in a related setting.

## Empirical Strategy

The core identification strategy exploits **within-individual (or within-household) variation** in village-level conflict exposure over time:

$$\text{Leave}_{iht} = \alpha_i + \delta_t + \gamma_{p(h)} \cdot t + \beta \cdot \text{Conflict}_{v(h)t} + \varepsilon_{iht}$$

where:

- $\alpha_i$ = **individual fixed effect** (absorbs all time-invariant heterogeneity)
- $\delta_t$ = **year fixed effect** (absorbs aggregate national trends)
- $\gamma_{p(h)} \cdot t$ = **province-specific time trend** (controls for differential regional trajectories)
- $\text{Conflict}_{v(h)t}$ = village-level conflict exposure (binary or continuous intensity)

Identification relies on whether the *same individual* migrates more in years when their village experiences conflict than in years without conflict.
