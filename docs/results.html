Main Regression Results and Coefficient Visualizations

Published

March 1, 2026

This page presents the main regression results. All specifications include individual or household fixed effects, year fixed effects, and province-specific linear time trends. Standard errors are clustered at the village (sous-colline) level. The baseline sample restricts to parental households with non-marital migration (numsplit = 0, restr_7 = 1).

The estimating equations are:

Individual-level (Eq. 1): \[\text{Leave}_{iht} = \alpha_i + \delta_t + \gamma_{p(h)} \cdot t + \beta \cdot \text{Conflict}_{v(h)t} + \varepsilon_{iht}\]

Household-level (Eq. 2): \[Y_{ht} = \mu_h + \delta_t + \gamma_{p(h)} \cdot t + \beta \cdot X_{ht} + \varepsilon_{ht}\]

Table 3 estimates Equation 1 for individual non-marital migration. Each column uses a different measure of conflict: village-level binary exposure (cols. 1), continuous intensity (col. 2), individual victimization types (cols. 3–7), and composite PCA indices (cols. 8–10).

Note

Interpretation — Column (1): A village-year with any conflict casualties increases the probability of non-marital migration by 3.1 percentage points (off a dep. var. mean of 4.5%). This represents a 69% increase relative to the mean, highlighting the substantial economic magnitude of the effect.

Non-Marital Migration (Household Level)

Table 4 estimates Equation 2 at the household level. The outcome is whether any household member migrated for non-marital reasons in year \(t\).

Note

Key finding: The asset PCA index (col. 9) has the same coefficient magnitude as village-level violence exposure (col. 1), suggesting that the economic disruption channel — specifically asset and income losses — is as important as the direct threat of violence in driving household migration responses.

Marital Migration

Tables 5 and 6 examine marital migration — the most common form of household dissolution in pre-war rural Burundi. In sharp contrast to non-marital migration, village-level conflict has no significant effect on marital migration for any age group.

While village violence does not drive marital migration, household-level asset losses do matter. The household-level analysis (Table 6) reveals that economic distress — particularly via the asset PCA index — increases the probability of a household sending a member to marry:

Return Migration

Table 7 examines whether conflict-induced migration is permanent or temporary. The dependent variable captures whether a previously migrated individual or household member returned home in year \(t\).

Important

Key finding — Permanence of displacement: Conflict intensity (column 2) reduces the probability of individual return by 34 percentage points per unit of the casualty rate. At the household level (column 4), the effect is −29 pp. Since the dep. var. mean is ~8–14%, these are large effects that imply civil war–induced household dissolution is largely permanent.

This has important implications for policy: interventions targeting conflict-affected households must account for the fact that migration-induced compositional changes are unlikely to self-reverse even after peace is restored.

### Visualizing the Mechanism

The pattern across columns reveals that the effect of village-level violence (cols. 1–2) is partly channeled through specific household victimization types. Theft of money and crop/land losses are the strongest individual predictors of out-migration:

```{r}
#| label: fig-coefficients-t3

#| fig-height: 4.5

# Coefficient plot from Table 3 estimates
coef_data <- tibble(
  variable = c(
    "Violence (exposure)", "Casualties/100",
    "Loss of land", "Theft of crops",
    "Theft of money", "Theft/dest. goods",
    "PCA: All shocks"
  ),
  estimate = c(0.031, 0.009, 0.024, 0.018, 0.031, 0.014, 0.021),
  se       = c(0.009, 0.004, 0.011, 0.008, 0.010, 0.008, 0.006),
  group    = c("Village level", "Village level",
               "HH victimization", "HH victimization",
               "HH victimization", "HH victimization",
               "PCA index")
) |>
  mutate(
    ci_lo = estimate - 1.96 * se,
    ci_hi = estimate + 1.96 * se,
    variable = fct_reorder(variable, estimate)
  )

ggplot(coef_data, aes(x = estimate, y = variable, color = group)) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = 0, linetype = "dashed", color = "gray60") +
  geom_point(size = 3.5) +
  geom_errorbarh(aes(xmin = ci_lo, xmax = ci_hi), height = 0.25, linewidth = 0.8) +

  scale_x_continuous(
    labels = scales::percent_format(accuracy = 1),
    limits = c(-0.005, 0.055)
  ) +
  labs(
    x = "Effect on non-marital migration probability (pp)",
    y = NULL,
    caption = "Note: Estimates from separate individual FE regressions (Table 3). 95% confidence intervals based on village-clustered SEs."
  ) +

  theme(
    legend.position = "bottom",
```
