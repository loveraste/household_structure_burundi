Survey Design

The Burundi Priority Household Survey (HPS)

The primary data source is the Burundi Priority Household Panel Survey (Enquête Prioritaire des Ménages), a three-wave longitudinal household survey covering rural Burundi.

Note

Full citation: Burundi Priority Household Panel Survey 1998–2007–2012, European Centre for Advanced Research in Economics and Statistics (ECARES), Université libre de Bruxelles. Data collected in collaboration with the World Bank and ISTEEBU (Institut de Statistiques et d’Études Économiques du Burundi).

Wave 1 — Baseline (1998): The first wave was conducted in 1998 by the World Bank and ISTEEBU. It covered 3,900 rural households in 390 rural clusters (sous-collines) plus 2,400 urban households, providing a nationally representative sample of rural Burundi. The survey collected detailed information on household composition, residence, health, education, economic activities and welfare, and child anthropometrics.

Wave 2 — First Follow-up (2007): The second wave, conducted in 2007, re-visited 100 randomly selected rural clusters from the original 390. Researchers successfully re-interviewed 872 original (parental) households and 536 split-off households (new households formed between waves by members of original households), for a total of 1,408 households. The 2007 follow-up also collected retrospective annual data on each household for the entire 1998–2007 period, including migration events, conflict exposure, and shock experiences. This retrospective structure is central to our identification strategy.

Wave 3 — Second Follow-up (2012): A third wave in 2012 re-interviewed 1,239 households, including a new module on intra-household decision-making administered to both spouses. This paper uses Waves 1 and 2 only.

Sample Attrition

The transition from Wave 1 (3,900 HHs) to Wave 2 (872 original HHs in the analytical sample) reflects two stages: (1) the sampling of 100 clusters from 390, and (2) within-cluster attrition over 1998–2007.

Conflict Data

The Burundian Civil War (1993–2005)

The Burundian civil war began on October 21, 1993, when President Melchior Ndadaye — Burundi’s first democratically elected president and first Hutu head of state — was assassinated in a coup attempt by Tutsi soldiers of the national army. His assassination triggered widespread ethnic violence across rural Burundi, pitting Hutu rebel groups against the Tutsi-led military government.

Important

Scale of the conflict: The war resulted in an estimated 300,000 deaths over the 1993–2005 period (and some estimates of 50,000–100,000 deaths in the first year alone), displaced over 1.2 million people, and caused Burundi’s GDP to contract by approximately 50% over the war period. Agriculture — employing over 90% of the rural population and contributing more than 40% to GDP — suffered severely from forced displacement, crop destruction, livestock theft, and land seizure.
