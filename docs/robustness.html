Robustness & Heterogeneity

Alternative Specifications, Placebo Tests, and Subgroup Analyses

Published

March 1, 2026

This page documents the robustness checks and heterogeneity analyses supporting the paper’s main findings. The baseline specification from the results page is:

\[\text{Leave}_{iht} = \alpha_i + \delta_t + \gamma_{p(h)} \cdot t + \beta \cdot \text{Conflict}_{v(h)t} + \varepsilon_{iht}\]

with individual FE (\(\alpha_i\)), year FE (\(\delta_t\)), province time trends, and village-clustered SEs.

Identification Assumptions

Province-Specific Time Trends

The province time trend \(\gamma_{p(h)} \cdot t\) is a key control in our specification. It absorbs any differential provincial-level trajectories that could spuriously correlate conflict with migration. In STATA, this was generated as bys province year: gen province_trend = _n; in R (fixest), it is implemented as province[year] interacted fixed effects.

Without this control, estimates could be biased if conflict-prone provinces were already experiencing higher (or lower) out-migration trends before conflict peaked. By including province × year FE, identification comes purely from within-province, within-year variation in conflict exposure.

Endogeneity Discussion

Note

Why conflict exposure is plausibly exogenous: The geography of the Burundian civil war was largely determined by: (1) the pre-war distribution of ethnic composition across sous-collines, (2) proximity to armed group strongholds and supply routes, and (3) military dynamics driven by national political factors.

These determinants are absorbed by individual and household fixed effects (time-invariant) or by province time trends (slow-moving regional factors). Individual migration decisions by a single household cannot plausibly affect village-level conflict intensity.

Placebo: Natural Shocks

A key identification concern is that village-level violence correlates with other types of local shocks (e.g., droughts, floods) that also drive migration. If natural shocks drive our results, their effects should be similar to those of conflict.

We test this by estimating the same fixed-effects specification using natural shock variables as the “treatment.” Under the null hypothesis that conflict uniquely drives migration (beyond general local hardship), natural shocks should have smaller and less significant effects.

Important

Interpretation: Natural shocks — rainfall, drought, crop disease, bad harvests — do not significantly increase migration in this setting. This supports the interpretation that the conflict effect captures something specific about violence and forced displacement, not merely a generic local economic shock.

Attrition and Selection

A concern with any panel study is that attrition between waves is non-random. If households that experienced more conflict violence were more likely to be lost from the panel, our estimates would understate the true effect of conflict on migration.

Verwimp & Bundervoet (2009) conduct a thorough attrition analysis and find that:

  1. No systematic selection on poverty: Attriting households do not differ significantly from remaining households in baseline poverty measures.
  2. No systematic selection on violence: Village-level conflict intensity in 1998–2006 is not significantly correlated with the probability of attrition.
  3. Attrition breakdown: 2.8% deceased, 2.5% moved-unknown, 2.4% disappeared, 3.6% dissolved, 1.3% unknown — totaling 12.6% attrition over 9 years.

The absence of violence-related attrition bias is particularly important for our identification, since it means we are not systematically missing the households most exposed to conflict.

Heterogeneity Analyses

We examine whether the effect of conflict on household migration varies across three key dimensions of household composition.

Appendix Table A1 — Adults vs. Children

Note

Research question: Does conflict affect migration of adult household members (≥18) differently from children (<18)? Adults are the primary income earners, so the economic disruption hypothesis predicts stronger adult migration. However, children may also be sent away for safety.

Finding: Both adults and children show positive migration responses to conflict, but adult responses are consistently larger. This is consistent with adults leaving primarily for economic reasons (income replacement), while child migration may be partly safety-driven.

Appendix Table A2 — Women vs. Men

Note

Research question: Are effects symmetric across gender? Standard models predict men may have lower migration costs (physical safety concerns, labor market access at destination), but women’s marital migration is analyzed separately and excluded from the baseline sample.

Finding: Effects are approximately equal for women and men in the non-marital migration sample. This is notable: conditional on excluding marriage, women and men are equally likely to leave the household in response to conflict.

Appendix Table A3 — Poor vs. Non-Poor Households

Note

Research question: Does baseline poverty moderate the conflict-migration relationship? Theory predicts two competing effects: (1) poor households may be more exposed to conflict-related income shocks and have fewer alternative coping mechanisms → stronger migration responses; (2) poor households may have higher migration costs (transport, information, social networks at destination) → weaker migration responses.

Finding: The poverty gradient is clear: effects of village violence and household victimization are consistently 2–3× larger for poor households compared to non-poor. This supports the first hypothesis — poor households have fewer alternative coping mechanisms and are more exposed to conflict-related income shocks. The poverty line used is BIF 8,173.15 per adult-equivalent (1998).
